# Methodology

## How I Approach Complex Problems

When I encounter a difficult problem, I rarely start by looking for a solution.

I start by trying to understand the system.

Many problems appear technical on the surface but are actually problems of organization, visibility, coordination, or decision-making.

My goal is to identify the underlying cause before deciding how to address it.

---

## Step 1: Understand the System

Before proposing solutions, I try to understand:

* What exists today?
* How does work currently flow?
* What constraints exist?
* What assumptions are being made?
* Where do people lose time or encounter difficulties?
* What information is difficult to locate or maintain?

I frequently use multiple LLMs during this stage to challenge assumptions, explore alternatives, and help identify blind spots.

The objective is not to find answers immediately.

The objective is to understand the problem well enough to ask better questions.

---

## Step 2: Identify the Real Problem

The first problem people describe is not always the actual problem.

Examples:

* "Debugging is slow."
* "Documentation is outdated."
* "Development takes too long."

Often these are symptoms.

I try to identify the underlying issue creating those symptoms.

Examples might include:

* Poor system visibility
* Unclear ownership
* Hidden dependencies
* Inconsistent organization
* Missing workflows

Solving the root cause is usually more effective than treating the symptoms.

---

## Step 3: Create Structure

Once the underlying problem is understood, I look for ways to introduce structure that improves how people interact with the system.

This may involve:

* Documentation
* Mapping
* Naming systems
* Dependency tracking
* Workflow design
* Decision frameworks
* Validation processes

The goal is to make complexity easier to manage rather than relying on individuals to remember everything.

---

## Step 4: Validate the Approach

Before investing heavily in a solution, I try to determine:

* Does it address the root problem?
* Does it remain useful as complexity grows?
* Does it introduce new problems?
* Can other people realistically use it?

Simple prototypes and thought experiments are often enough to identify major weaknesses early.

---

## Step 5: Use AI to Accelerate Development

I frequently use LLMs as design and implementation partners.

My role is typically focused on:

* Defining objectives
* Designing architecture
* Establishing structure
* Evaluating tradeoffs
* Reviewing outputs

The models help accelerate implementation, exploration, and iteration.

The goal is not automation for its own sake.

The goal is increasing effectiveness while maintaining human oversight.

---

## Step 6: Iterate and Refine

Most systems improve through repeated use.

As new information becomes available, I refine:

* Structure
* Workflows
* Validation
* Documentation
* Decision processes

The objective is continuous improvement rather than a one-time solution.

---

# Principles

### Organization Before Optimization

Poorly organized systems rarely become good systems through optimization alone.

Improving structure often creates larger gains than improving speed.

### Visibility Creates Better Decisions

People make better decisions when dependencies, constraints, and tradeoffs are visible.

### Systematic Processes Scale

Repeatable processes generally scale better than relying on individual expertise or institutional knowledge.

### Complexity Should Be Manageable

Complex systems are inevitable.

Unmanageable complexity is not.

### AI Works Best With Structure

The more clearly information, context, and objectives are organized, the more effectively humans and AI can work together.
